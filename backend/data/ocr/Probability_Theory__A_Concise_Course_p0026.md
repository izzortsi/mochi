given events $A_1$, $A_2$ and $A$, $A_1 \cup A_2$ is the union of the sets $A_1$ and $A_2$, $A_1 \cap A_2$ is the intersection of the sets $A_1$ and $A_2$, $\bar{A} = \Omega - A$ is the complement of the set $A$ relative to the whole space $\Omega$, and so on. Thus the symbols $\cup$, $\cap$, etc. have their customary set-theoretic meaning. Moreover, the statement that “the occurrence of the event $A_1$ implies that of the event $A_2$” (or simply, “$A_1$ implies $A_2$”) means that $A_1 \subset A_2$, i.e., that the set $A_1$ is a subset of the set $A_2$.

To visualize relations between events, it is convenient to represent the sample space $\Omega$ schematically by some plane region and the elementary events $\omega$ by points in this region. Then events, i.e., sets of points $\omega$, become various plane figures. Thus Figure 2 shows various relations between two events $A_1$ and $A_2$, represented by circular disks lying inside a rectangle $\Omega$, schematically representing the whole sample space. In turn, this way of representing events in terms of plane figures can be used to deduce general relations between events, e.g.,

a) If $A_1 \subset A_2$, then $\bar{A}_1 \supset \bar{A}_2$;
b) If $A = A_1 \cup A_2$, then $\bar{A} = \bar{A}_1 \cap \bar{A}_2$;
c) If $A = A_1 \cap A_2$, then $\bar{A} = \bar{A}_1 \cup \bar{A}_2$.

$^3$ The symbol $\subset$ means “is a subset of” or “is contained in,” while $\supset$ means “contains.”
