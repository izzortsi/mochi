**Total Boundedness**

The Heine-Borel Theorem states that a subset of $\mathbb{R}^m$ is compact if and only if it is closed and bounded. In more general metric spaces, such as $\mathbb{Q}$, the assertion is false. But what if the metric space is complete? As remarked on page 81 it is still false.

But mathematicians do not quit easily. The Heine-Borel Theorem ought to generalize beyond $\mathbb{R}^m$ somehow. Here is the concept we need: A set $A \subset M$ is **totally bounded** if for each $\epsilon > 0$ there exists a finite covering of $A$ by $\epsilon$-neighborhoods. No mention is made of a covering reducing to a subcovering. How close total boundedness is to the worthless fact that every metric space has a finite open covering!

**65 Generalized Heine-Borel Theorem** A subset of a complete metric space is compact if and only if it is closed and totally bounded.
